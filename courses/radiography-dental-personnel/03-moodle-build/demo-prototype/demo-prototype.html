X-Ray Tube Anatomy

The anatomy of the X-ray tubehead dictates every exposure you make. This lesson builds the mental model you'll use for the rest of the course.

Lesson overview

You'll learn how electrons are generated, accelerated, and converted into X-ray photons — and why knowing this changes the way you set kVp and mA at chairside.

Inside the Tubehead — Electron to Photon

12:04 · captions + transcript available

Core concepts

Thermionic emission

The cathode's tungsten filament heats up when current is applied, ejecting a cloud of electrons. Without this "boil-off," there is no source for the beam.

kVp and mA — what each controls

kVp controls electron speed and therefore the energy of the beam — more penetrating power, more contrast differentiation. mA controls how many electrons are produced — more mA, more X-ray photons, denser image.

i

Key concept

A higher kVp does not just mean a "brighter" image. It changes the contrast scale — a 70 kVp image has shorter gray-scale (sharper black-to-white), while 90 kVp softens contrast and shows more subtle density differences.

✚

Clinical tip

When you troubleshoot a "too dark" image, don't reach for kVp first. In 8 out of 10 chairside cases, the fix is mA or exposure time — and you preserve diagnostic contrast by leaving kVp alone.

!

Compliance alert · FAC 64B5-9.011

Non-licensed personnel may expose dental radiographs only under the supervision of a Florida-licensed dentist, and must have completed approved training. You are training now — documentation of your completion becomes part of your Board-of-Dentistry-auditable record.

Think about the last radiograph you assisted with. Did the operator explain their kVp/mA choice? Could you explain it now? Jot your answer in your Module 01 notebook — you'll return to this in Lesson 3.

Scenario 1 of 3
Adult bitewing · suspected overlap
Multiple Choice

Your operator took a premolar bitewing on a 45-year-old patient. The resulting image shows clear contact overlap between the upper second premolar and upper first molar. The operator asks you what most likely went wrong.

What is the most likely cause of the overlap?

A
Insufficient kVp — the beam did not penetrate enough to separate the structures.
B
Horizontal angulation error — the beam was not directed through the contact space.
C
The patient moved during exposure.
D
The receptor was placed too close to the lingual surface.
✓

Correct — Horizontal angulation

Overlap on bitewings is almost always a horizontal-angulation problem: the central ray must pass through the contact space parallel to the interproximal surfaces. Patient movement typically produces blur, not overlap. kVp and receptor placement affect density and coverage, not contact separation.

Procedure steps

Confirm prescription

Read the prescription back to the supervising dentist. Confirm the series (FMX / bitewings / periapical) and any patient-specific modifications before touching equipment.

Place barriers

Cover the tubehead, control panel, and sensor. Use barrier tape on any surface that will contact a contaminated glove during the procedure.

Verify machine settings

Set kVp, mA, and exposure time per the prescription. Read the settings back aloud — this is a double-check your supervising dentist can hear.

Seat and shield the patient

Lead apron first, thyroid collar second. Confirm the collar is flat and at the chin line, not pushed up or tucked under.

Position and expose

Operator positions the tubehead. Step behind the shielded wall or the recommended distance. Verify the patient is still and expose.
